> **Intro**  
> The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) is drafting a significant proposal: making the mandatory quarterly filing requirement for listed companies optional in favor of a semi‑annual report. This initiative reflects a reassessment of the “short‑sighted” culture in traditional capital markets and coincides with a pivotal moment when public‑blockchain (public blockchain) ecosystems are beginning to attract institutional capital. Below we examine the policy’s drivers, market implications, strategic shifts for institutional investors, and the attendant risks—offering a comprehensive view of this regulatory‑and‑blockchain crossroads.  

## 1. Policy Drivers: From “Short‑Termism” to “Long‑Term Value”

- **Short‑term earnings pressure** – U.S. public companies must file Form 10‑K/10‑Q every quarter, prompting management to make “quarter‑beat” decisions aimed at meeting market expectations.  
- **Regulatory efficiency** – Internal SEC analysis estimates that audit and compliance costs tied to quarterly reporting consume roughly 5 % of a company’s total expenses, while delivering limited incremental value to investors.  
- **Alignment with public‑chain governance** – Most public‑blockchain projects (e.g., Ethereum, Polkadot) operate on six‑month or longer governance cycles for upgrades and audits. Reducing filing frequency helps synchronise disclosure rhythms between traditional finance and blockchain projects.  

## 2. Direct Impact on Traditional Financial Markets  

### 2.1 Transparency – A Double‑Edged Sword  

| Dimension                | Current Quarterly Reporting | Semi‑Annual Reporting (Proposed) |
|--------------------------|-----------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Update frequency         | Every 3 months              | Every 6 months                   |
| Decision‑making basis    | Short‑term performance spikes | Emphasis on long‑term trends |
| Compliance cost          | Relatively high            | Potential ~30 % reduction        |
| Information‑asymmetry risk | Lower                     | May increase                     |

- **Mitigating short‑term volatility** – A six‑month reporting window dampens market over‑reactions to single‑quarter “earnings surprises,” reducing abrupt price swings.  
- **Rising information asymmetry** – Longer intervals between updates mean investors have fewer real‑time data points, potentially delaying the market’s perception of internal risks.  

### 2.2 Institutional Investor Strategy Adjustments  

Institutional players (hedge funds, pension funds, sovereign wealth funds) typically employ multi‑factor models for asset allocation. A shift in reporting cadence forces these models to incorporate **information timeliness** as an explicit factor. Expected behavioural changes include:  

- **Higher allocation to deep‑dive fundamental research** – Greater reliance on detailed financial analysis, sector outlooks, and management interviews.  
- **Extended holding horizons** – Moving from intra‑quarteral tactical trades to semi‑annual or multi‑year value‑oriented positions.  

## 3. Accelerating Institutionalization of the Public‑Chain Ecosystem  

### 3.1 Compliance Advantages from Synchronous Disclosure  

Public‑chain projects have gradually introduced **compliance disclosure frameworks** (e.g., the Ethereum Foundation’s financial transparency reports). With the SEC softening reporting frequency, traditional financial institutions will find the disclosure cadence of public chains more compatible, lowering cross‑domain compliance costs.  

### 3.2 New Capital‑Inflow Pathways  

- **Fund‑level positioning** – Institutional funds can categorize public‑chain projects as “long‑term infrastructure” assets, using the semi‑annual window for systematic audits and due‑diligence.  
- **Expansion of derivatives markets** – Longer disclosure cycles will require re‑calibration of pricing models for options, futures, and other derivatives, opening space for innovative contract structures on exchanges.  

## 4. Risks and Regulatory Challenges  

1. **Manipulation risk from delayed disclosures** – With a larger lag between data releases, insiders might trade on material, non‑public information. The SEC must concurrently strengthen **insider‑trading surveillance** mechanisms.  
2. **Cross‑border regulatory divergence** – The U.S. may adopt semi‑annual reporting while the EU, Asia‑Pacific regulators retain quarterly mandates, potentially spawning arbitrage opportunities and compliance friction.  
3. **Governance risk in public‑chain projects** – Although the reporting cadence aligns with blockchain governance cycles, public chains still face technical upgrade uncertainty. Institutional investors must monitor roadmap feasibility and community consensus health.  

> **Key Takeaway**: Looser reporting requirements do **not** equate to lax enforcement. Companies must still ensure the accuracy, completeness, and timeliness of disclosed information. When entering the public‑chain arena, institutional investors should conduct comprehensive assessments of governance structures, technical implementation paths, and audit capabilities.  

## 5. Closing Thoughts & Investor Risk Notice  

The SEC’s proposal signals a shift in U.S. capital markets from a “short‑term earnings‑driven” mindset toward “long‑term value creation.” The institutionalization of public‑blockchain projects dovetails neatly with this evolution. For traditional corporations, semi‑annual reports could cut compliance costs and free up strategic bandwidth; for the crypto ecosystem, they provide a smoother bridge to conventional finance.  

**Risk Notice**  
- Reduced disclosure frequency may heighten information asymmetry; investors should scrutinise corporate governance quality and auditor independence.  
- Public‑chain projects remain in rapid‑iteration phases; technical and regulatory uncertainties persist.  
- Divergent reporting regimes across jurisdictions could trigger arbitrage and compliance conflicts; multi‑jurisdictional deployments should be accompanied by rigorous legal review.  

With regulation and technology propelling each other forward, the future capital market will increasingly fuse blockchain innovation with established financial institutions. Investors who grasp the policy direction, understand public‑chain governance, and perform diligent risk analysis stand to capture long‑term value in this transformative wave.
